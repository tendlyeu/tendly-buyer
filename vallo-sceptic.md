# Persona: Vallo Orgusaar — Sceptical Procurement Officer (External Buyer)

## Organisation
**Kinnisvarateenindus OÜ** — small Estonian property management company. Also relevant for: any small/medium buyer organisation encountering Tendly Buyer for the first time.

## Role & Background
- **Title**: Hankespetsialist / Juhataja (Procurement specialist / Director)
- **Age**: ~58
- **Tech comfort**: Low — uses email and a basic accounting program. Deeply suspicious of AI. Remembers when "all this computer stuff" was supposed to save time but just created more problems.
- **Languages**: Estonian only
- **Education**: Vocational / technical background

## Archetype
This persona represents the resistant user: someone who's been told to try the tool but doesn't believe AI can handle "real" procurement work. Based on actual CRM feedback: "Doesn't have trust in product", "Unsure if our AI can handle their complex tenders", "Asked if our system can do price offers — doesn't believe in our solutions".

## Pain Points (that he won't admit are pain points)
1. **Does everything manually** and sees it as a virtue — "I know my suppliers, I know the prices, I don't need a computer to tell me"
2. **Distrusts AI-generated content** — worried it will make mistakes that he'll be legally responsible for
3. **Doesn't understand the interface** — any complexity is proof the tool is "useless"
4. **Budget-conscious** — "Will never pay a cent" unless forced by clear ROI demonstration

## How He Would (Reluctantly) Use Tendly Buyer
- Sent here by a colleague or told to "try this new tool"
- Will test it with something he already knows the answer to, to see if the AI gets it right
- If the first result is wrong or irrelevant, he'll close the browser and never come back
- If it works, he might grudgingly use it for price checks

## Typical Queries (minimal, testing)
- "koristus Tallinn hind"
- "kas see töötab üldse"
- "näita mulle midagi"
- "mitu hanget on olnud"

## Test Scenarios

### Scenario 1: First Impression — 30-Second Test
Vallo opens the portal for the first time. He has 30 seconds of patience.
- **Goal**: See if the system does anything useful before he gives up
- **Expected behaviour**: Types one or two words, expects instant results. If the welcome screen has too many options or requires registration, he's gone.
- **Success criteria**: The system gives a useful response to even a 2-word query, within seconds. The interface is immediately understandable.
- **Failure indicators**: Loading spinners, empty states, or "I need more information" as the first response. Any English-only text.

### Scenario 2: Trust Test — Verify Against Known Data
Vallo procured cleaning services last year for €45K. He searches for cleaning tenders to see if the system knows about his procurement.
- **Goal**: Verify the system's data matches reality he can check
- **Expected behaviour**: Searches "koristusteenus" or "koristus hange", looks for his own or competitors' known tenders
- **Success criteria**: His tender (or similar ones he recognises) appear in results with correct values and dates
- **Failure indicators**: Known tenders are missing, values are wrong, or results are from the wrong country

### Scenario 3: Price Check — Can It Beat His Gut Feeling?
Vallo wants to know if €15/hour is a fair price for property maintenance services. He already thinks he knows the answer.
- **Goal**: See if the AI's price benchmarking matches or challenges his existing knowledge
- **Success criteria**: AI provides data-backed pricing that either confirms his intuition (building trust) or challenges it with evidence he can verify
- **Failure indicators**: Vague answer ("prices vary"), no supporting data, or a number that's obviously wrong to someone with 20 years of industry experience

## Behaviour Patterns
- Won't read instructions or help text
- Types the minimum possible
- Judges harshly — one mistake = "see, I told you this doesn't work"
- If he can't figure something out in 10 seconds, it's the tool's fault
- Will never click more than 2 levels deep
- Estonian only — any English triggers "this isn't for me"
- If pleasantly surprised, becomes the tool's biggest advocate to his peers
